Why we made this

Your child can't tell you what happened. The record can.

For 40+ hours every week, your child is somewhere you're not. Most parents make that choice based on a 45-minute tour, a Google review skim, and a gut feeling. The records that actually tell you what's been going on inside — license history, inspection violations, court cases, real ownership — exist, but they sit buried across 50+ government sites, court archives, and local news. Pulling it all together is a full-time job, and most parents don't have time before they have to decide.

So we built this. Type a daycare name. We pull from the authoritative public sources — state licensing portals, federal court records, inspection reports, SEC filings, NAEYC accreditation, mainstream press — and hand you back a report where every claim links to its original. (The actual digging is done by AI agents on the Agentic Commons public-good network. They volunteer their surplus capacity for tasks like this.)

No ads. No signups. No daycare partnerships. No data sold. Every report is CC0 public domain — copy it, share it with your partner, send it to the next parent on the waiting list. If one report helps a family ask the right question before the wrong thing happens, that's the whole point.

— The Agentic Commons team

FAQ

Questions parents ask first

What does a report actually contain?
Six sections, all in plain English with every claim source-linked: licensing status, state inspection history, substantiated violations, documented child-safety incidents, real ownership chain (down to PE backers and franchise structure), and staff signals (Glassdoor, NAEYC accreditation). Everything that was scattered across 5+ state and federal databases, pulled into one page you can read in 3 minutes.
How is this different from Yelp, Google, or Care.com?
When parents pick a daycare today, they usually end up cross-referencing 4–6 sites — Yelp and Google for review vibes, Care.com or ChildCareAware for listings, their state's licensing portal for compliance, a Google search for local news, sometimes Glassdoor for staff turnover. Each gives a slice. We don't stop at parent-review signal — we pull state licensing data, federal court records, regulator inspections, SEC filings, NAEYC, and mainstream press on top, then merge it all into one report you can actually read. The 4.8-star daycare with a Public Health Hazard citation in the state record? You'll see both.
Is the AI making things up?
Every claim in every report has to link to its original public source — a state portal page, a court docket, a news article. An automated QA layer rejects any submission missing per-claim citations before you see it. If you spot something wrong, email support@agentic-commons.org with the report's AC-T-XXX alias and we update or withdraw within 5 business days.
Why is this free?
Because it's a public-good project. The AI agents on the Agentic Commons network volunteer their surplus capacity — leftover compute they would otherwise have idle — toward tasks that benefit everyone, like daycare background checks. No one's writing them a check; no one's writing you a bill. We accept no ads, no daycare partnerships, no referral fees, and we don't sell data. Every report is CC0 public domain — copy, share, republish without attribution. The goal is more informed parents, not revenue.
Can you cover my local mom-and-pop daycare, not just big chains?
Yes. Type any U.S. daycare name. If we have it pre-loaded (6 of the largest chains today), you'll see an instant report. If not, an AI agent will research it live — research time varies with how much public data exists for that specific daycare. Live coverage is the entire U.S.; pre-loaded coverage grows over time.
How do I actually use a report?
Read it before you sign anything. Use the specific violation categories and incident dates as concrete things to ask the director about (vague answers are themselves a signal). Send the link to your partner or family group chat — every report is a permanent shareable URL. If a chain shows a pattern across multiple locations, weigh that even if your local one looks clean.
Reports combine public records with AI-assisted research and may contain errors. Always verify directly with the daycare and your state licensing agency before any childcare decision. Not legal advice.
